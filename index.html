About

I am a fourth-year PhD candidate in Economic Geography at the London School of Economics, funded by the Economic and Social Research Council. My supervisors are Riccardo Crescenzi and Vassilis Monastiriotis.

My research examines how regions can harness Foreign Direct Investment and integration into Global Value Chains to stimulate local economic development, build resilience to shocks and uncertainty, and support the transition to a greener economy. I am particularly interested in the role of regional institutions - especially Investment Promotion Agencies - in shaping whether foreign investments prove transformational for local economies.

My background is in Economics & Geography, having completed a BSc in Economics & Geography from University College London, plus an MSc in Local Economic Development from the London School of Economics. Before starting my PhD, I worked as a policy analyst at Policy in Practice and as a consultant for the World Bank. Alongisde my PhD, I have worked as a Research Officer on the EU Horizon-funded ESSPIN project, leading a Europe-wide survey of Investment Promotion Agencies. I am currently also the Co-PI on an International Growth Centre (IGC) funded research project to measure and understand patterns of green FDI/GVCs into Africa.

Research Interests

Working Papers

Can Regional Institutions Protect FDI During Uncertainty? Evidence from Brexit
Job market paper — Draft available
This paper investigates whether Investment Promotion Agencies' sector-targeting strategies protected inward FDI during the Brexit uncertainty period (2016–2019). Using a difference-in-differences design, I find that pre-2015 IPA targeting increased repeat knowledge-intensive services FDI, with effects concentrated in regions outside South East England and among investments motivated by regional ecosystem factors. These findings suggest that institutional effectiveness during uncertainty operates through ecosystem-building rather than information provision.

Building a New Atlas of Green Foreign Investment Beyond Renewables
With Riccardo Crescenzi and Lee Mager — In preparation
Using Large Language Models in combination with the EU Taxonomy of sustainable activities, we develop a comprehensive classification of green FDI projects that extends beyond renewable energy, capturing the full breadth of investments contributing to the low-carbon transition.
Going for green: the impact of Investment Promotion Agencies on growing green FDI
With Riccardo Crescenzi and Marco Di Cataldo — In preparation
This paper examines whether Investment Promotion Agencies can attract green foreign direct investment through targeted sector strategies. Combining novel data on IPA targeting with a new classification of green FDI that extends beyond renewables, we investigate whether pre-existing IPA strategies to promote green sectors shaped the geography of green investment flows into European regions. The findings speak to whether place-based industrial policy can support the green transition by steering foreign investment toward low-carbon activities.
Beyond Attraction: Does Investment Promotion Work for FDI Retention and Expansion?
With Riccardo Crescenzi, Marco Di Cataldo & Mara Giua — In preparation
While most research on Investment Promotion Agencies focuses on their role in attracting new foreign investors, this paper examines whether IPAs also influence the behaviour of established multinational enterprises. Using project-level data on FDI expansions and divestments across European regions, we investigate whether IPA activities affect the retention and growth of existing foreign investments — a channel that remains largely unexplored despite the increasing importance of repeat investment in global FDI flows.
Beyond Information Desks: How Investment Promotion Agencies Build Regional Ecosystems
In preparation
This paper uses qualitative evidence from in-depth interviews with Investment Promotion Agency professionals across European regions to understand how these institutions operate in practice. Moving beyond the standard framing of IPAs as information providers, I am exploring how agencies coordinate with universities, local governments, and incumbent firms to build the regional ecosystems that attract and embed foreign investors. The findings reveal substantial heterogeneity in institutional capabilities and strategies, with implications for understanding when and how place-based investment promotion proves effective.
